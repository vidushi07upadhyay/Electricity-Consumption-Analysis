Key Insights

Discover the major findings from India's electricity consumption data spanning over two decades.

Exponential Growth

India's electricity consumption has grown by over 156% since 2000, reaching 1,400+ TWh in 2023. This reflects rapid industrialization, urbanization, and improved rural electrification.

Regional Disparities

Maharashtra, Gujarat, and Tamil Nadu lead in consumption, while northeastern states lag significantly. Western and southern regions account for over 50% of national demand.

Industrial Dominance

The industrial sector remains the largest consumer at 42%, followed by domestic (25%), agriculture (18%), and commercial (10%) sectors showing evolving patterns.

Residential Surge

Domestic electricity consumption has tripled since 2000, driven by increased appliance ownership, air conditioning adoption, and work-from-home trends post-2020.

COVID-19 Impact

2020 saw a historic 3.2% decline in demand during lockdowns. However, 2021-2023 witnessed strong recovery with 8.4% annual growth, surpassing pre-pandemic levels.

Future Outlook

Projections indicate consumption will double by 2030, driven by EV adoption, industrial expansion, and continued electrification efforts across rural India.

About This Project

This interactive data analytics project explores India's electricity consumption patterns from 2019 to 2020. Using data from the Central Electricity Authority, the dashboard provides comprehensive insights into national trends, regional variations, and sector-wise breakdowns that shape India's energy landscape.

Key Features

  • Interactive state-wise consumption map with hover details
  • Time-series analysis showing 2 years of consumption trends
  • Sector breakdown with industry, residential, and agriculture data
  • COVID-19 impact analysis with pre and post-lockdown comparisons

Data Sources

All data has been sourced from official publications by the Central Electricity Authority (CEA), Ministry of Power, Government of India. The visualizations are built using Tableau Public.
